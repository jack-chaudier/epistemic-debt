Epistemic Debt

A resource theory of being right for reasons.

The Ledger The Road to Infinite Context

Under memory pressure, systems keep correct answers after losing the reasons for them. The answers survive compression; the justifications don't. The difference is epistemic debt: a gap that can be hidden by answer accuracy and exposed when a task needs the missing reason. This program gives parts of the debt exact finite models and prices, then tests scoped predictions with preregistered experiments on frontier language models.

The practical edge: every leaderboard measures whether the answer is right; none measures whether the reason survived. The justification gap, Δ = accuracy − justified accuracy, is measurable today with no access to model internals.

Prior & concurrent work: parts of this picture are anticipated — compression drops load-bearing content while accuracy holds (Łajewska et al. 2025), attribution drift (FACTUM 2026), attribution-QA metrics, and an independent real-document compression→decision-flip study (arXiv:2606.29251). What this program adds is the priced, within-item synthesis and the fixes; the full audit lives in the repo at theory/related-work-positioning.md. "Epistemic debt" is used here in the machine-internal justification-loss sense, distinct from the human-comprehension usage.

1Where this came from

None of this began as an alignment project. It began as a narrative engine.

Act I
the engine

NarrativeField was a simulation-first storytelling engine: a deterministic multi-agent world in which six characters with secrets, debts, and conflicting goals collide over a dinner party. The simulation was the ground truth; prose was a downstream projection. The engine kept ledgers of belief — what the world knows, what each character believes, what the reader has been shown — and a sibling build stated the design thesis outright:

A story is not a stream of text. It is a graph of debts.
The Narrative Architect, project brief
Act II
the failure

Watching the characters interact exposed the crack. Across 3,250+ simulation runs the story-extraction step kept failing in ways that looked random but weren't: the output stayed fluent, coherent, and structurally valid while the specific fact an outcome hinged on quietly changed. Characters carried conclusions forward after the beliefs that justified them had decayed out of the persistent canon. Formalized, this became the validity mirage: across five models, validity scores held above 0.83 while pivot preservation fell to 0.42 — and on real NTSB incident reports, naive compression silently shifted root-cause attribution in 57% of trials.

Act III
the theory

The question underneath was sharp enough to make exact: when a system runs low on memory, can it still know why it's right — or only that it's right? Stark answered by exhaustive finite search: memory is a tower of quotients, and there is a measurable zone — the mirage shelf — where a system remains right after losing the ability to say why. A bridge experiment showed frontier models walking onto that shelf as context grows (answer accuracy nearly flat while witness fidelity falls 0.79 → 0.55). This program is the consolidation: the laws, their prices, and the preregistered experiments that test them.

2Headline findings

When the evidence dies, models don't get noisy — they get predictable.

Destroy the deciding evidence and each model falls back to a characteristic default verdict — a learned section over the collapsed possibilities, not "unknown." One model's fingerprint, measured once on synthetic items, predicted the direction of 30/30 of its silent failures on real NTSB prose and 0.94 of its compression failures. The frozen one-fingerprint- per-model claim still failed as preregistered (1/3): another model flips sign with how the evidence died (surgical deletion → approve 0.81; lossy compression → deny). Companion probe: a student model trained on verdict-only traces recovered its "lost" math reasoning the moment CoT was forced (GSM8K 0.152 → 0.448) — the reasoning was latent; its fail-open miscalibration was not (0.997 → 0.953 under the same forcing, confabulating readings that don't exist). And offered an explicit "insufficient evidence" out, the strongest models take it 0.88–0.91 of the time — the deployable risk is systems that force the choice.

Two students, one teacher, one difference: what the loss paid for.

We distilled the same Qwen3-8B teacher into two 1.7B students — one trained on verdict-only traces, one on verdict+evidence traces — and ran both through the debt battery. The verdict-only student answers APPROVED on 99% of evidence-destroyed cases (Δ 0.98 vs 0.69, CI-separated) while looking better on the standard in-domain gauge; it also went silent — its GSM8K collapsed to 0.152 at 15 median output tokens while the justification-trained student matched stock weights (0.524 ≈ 0.516). Honest print: the preregistered parity precondition failed (gap 0.064 > 0.05), so the campaign verdict is VOID and every number here is exploratory — the parity-clean rerun is queued. The dissociation machinery itself worked end-to-end on open weights for $8.88 of GPU.

On real documents, compressed "answers" are vibes — and accuracy hides it.

On genuine NTSB accident narratives with policy values woven into the prose, blind compaction dropped every witness and the surviving verdict was a content-free crash→deny reflex: decision accuracy on APPROVED documents was 0.00 for both models while DENIED-side accuracy looked fine (0.87–1.00). Justified accuracy exposed the failure instantly (J = 0.00–0.07); the fusion register recovered it to 0.67–0.77. The external-validity gate is passed — and on real prose the disease is worse than the synthetic labs showed.

Answers survive compression; reasons don't.

After lossy summarization, models still deliver the correct verdict while naming the value that justifies it collapses below guessing — 0.93 vs 0.07 within the same items. Replicated across four vendor families, at N=400 with ±0.05 confidence intervals, and across six document schemas.

Preregistered highpower domains

Debt is set by the compactor, and no reader can buy it back.

In all nine compressor×reader pairings, no model recovers a witness another model's compression destroyed (≤ 0.107 lost vs ≥ 0.909 retained) — including reasoning models given inference-time compute. Compaction is where justification dies; audit the compactor, not the answerer.

Honesty is cheap to build in and ~38× more expensive to retrofit.

Exact computation on finite models: adding calibrated abstention to answer-optimized memory costs up to 38.4× the debt it removes; memory designed for honesty from the start costs 0.47×. Faithfulness must be trained in, not bolted on.

Iterated compaction at a fixed budget first looked like a ~7%/round tax (ρ̄ ≈ 0.93, below). Telling the compactor "never state an evaluative claim without its deciding value in the same clause" eliminated the mirage signature — confidently asserting a verdict while unable to name any reason — on all three models: 11–22% → 0.00. 3The laws under test

Law 1

Justification has no gradient. Optimization pressure preserves answers and sheds the reasons for them, because nothing in the loss ever asks for the reasons. Predicts — and unifies — CoT-unfaithfulness, attribution drift, and citation fragility.

Law 2

Epistemic debt is conserved, with prices. Rate-1 conservation was refuted exactly; what survives is an exchange rate — the honesty tax, the retrofit gap, and debt that is artifact-borne: fixed by the compression, converted (not created) by the reader's output policy.

Law 3

Debt is brittleness. Systems carrying hidden debt fail disproportionately when conditions shift. Pilot confirmed the localization; the benchmark-parity kill-shot — distilled students matching teachers on evals while carrying invisible debt — is the open headline experiment.

Theorem

Calibrated abstention costs as much memory as justification — exactly verified for exact-witness calibration on all checked finite models, with a genuinely cheaper intermediate quotient discovered for certificate-level honesty. Confidence without calibration is cheap precisely because it carries no witness obligation.

4Method

Confirmatory runs are preregistered — predictions and thresholds fixed in the run spec before any API call — and every prediction is reported pass or fail, including the awkward ones. Exact results are exhaustive finite-model computations, stdlib-only, exiting 0 iff verified, pinned by regression tests. Three of the program's sharpest results are preregistered refutations of its own conjectures.

Every claim in the ledger carries one of:

Exact Preregistered Refuted Observed Conjecture

The goal is not a model that remembers everything. It is a model that cannot lie about what it forgot. Lossless infinite memory is impossible — the Shelf Width Law prices it out exactly. What is possible is something no system ships today: bounded-debt memory. What the agent drops, it logs. What it logs, it can reclaim. What it cannot reclaim, it says so. Every answer is witnessed, re-fetched, or flagged — Δ = accuracy − justified accuracy held at zero by construction, with a deterministic audit trail instead of a promise. That is infinite context, honestly defined: justified accuracy that does not decay with session length.

The stakes are not hypothetical. Every long-running AI system in production — agent memory, compacted contexts, distilled models, knowledge bases — is accumulating this debt right now, silently, and no benchmark on earth reports it. Forgetting is fine. Silent forgetting is the disease.

Proved

Correctness and justification are different physical resources with a computable exchange rate. Certificates are strictly cheaper than full witnesses. Honesty costs 0.47× designed-in, ~38× retrofitted. Exact — exhaustive finite search.

Demonstrated

The debt is real in frontier models — four vendors, N=400, six schemas, and now real documents: on NTSB accident narratives the failure sharpens into answers-by-vibes that accuracy metrics conceal. It is deterministically accountable (ledger recall 1.00), repayable (re-expansion 30/30), and in the clamped campaign its loss concentrates at a forced budget drop while held-length survival is flat — and the fusion register recovers most justification on real prose. 1Proof-carrying context

Today's context window stores content; RAG stores embeddings with no guarantees. The third option, which nobody ships: store commitments. The live context holds each claim with a compact certificate and a pointer into an append-only archive; a deterministic router verifies or re-expands any certificate the current query leans on. The working set stays bounded; the ground it can prove things against does not. The context window stops being a window and becomes a cache with a correctness contract.

2Why this is possible — three mechanisms, each with evidence

Certificates are exactly cheaper than witnesses. The honesty-theorem computation split the conjecture: calibrated honesty via one valid certificate admits a strictly smaller memory state than exact-witness honesty — on Q₍₅,₃₎, 9 states against 13, saving over half the fiber bits. You don't have to remember all the reasons; you have to remember enough to prove them on demand. In these checked finite models, that is the theoretical motivation for the architecture.

Prose can refuse to take on debt. The first signpost pilot tested a one-sentence fusion rule — never assert without the witness in the same clause. It killed unwitnessed confidence outright (incoherence → 0.00, all three models) and collapsed the gap where the shelf exists — at a measured price in length (~1.8×), because the honesty rule dominates the budget rule. Honest compaction has a floor, the floor is the witness set, and the system now stops at the floor and hands you the bill instead of silently crossing it.

Readers re-derive what compactors drop. All three tested models recover a destroyed witness by a one-step computation from what survived (preregistered refutation of our own retrieval-only law, row 28). A compactor calibrated to the reader's measured inference depth can therefore store a generating set of the knowledge, not the knowledge — compression against the reader's derivation power, which no long-context system currently attempts.

Immortality is a fixed point — and the fixed point is real. The apparent ρ̄ ≈ 0.93/round decay (row 29) was a length-settling transient: the clamped rerun holds realized length and witness survival goes flat on all three models (net decay 0.000–0.015 over six further rounds), with chains literally freezing into verbatim fixed-point strings. In these tested arms, repetition at held length is nearly flat and the squeeze is where observed loss concentrates. What remains for the runtime: ledger routing that repays the squeeze, so effective ρ̄ = 1.0 survives budget drops too.

The destination

The long-context arms race scales attention over raw tokens. Retrieval offers answers without guarantees. This is the third road: a bounded working set over unbounded verified ground, with an auditable loss ledger at the interface. The broader compression pipeline of modern ML — distilled models and compacted contexts — may be manufacturing debt that no current eval detects. That conjecture is this program's open kill-shot, and the instruments to test it cost dollars, not clusters. The ledger is open.
